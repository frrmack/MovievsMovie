Rating movies is hard

Research has shown that when we rate movies, the rating depends a lot on what you've seen (or rated) lately, what your mood is, which other film experiences come to mind when you try to translate your enjoyment to a grade (out of 4, 5, 10, 100 or any scale). Our memories cannot evalute the entirety of all movies we have seen, so every time we rate a movie, even though it feels like an ultimate rating, we are really comparing it to a limited set. As an example, if we are rating a whole bunch of movies in succession, if they are all movies we are fond of, our grading becomes a lot more strict, without us noticing it. This is because the human brain is great at adaptation, pattern recognition, and setting a baseline. This is generally very useful, but in this case it becomes harder to find out which movies you truly like best, or to have a complete, continuous ranking that doesn't depend on different conditions of each rating session.

Start the Fights

Star rating as a start

A star rating (1-5 stars out of 5) is a good starting point to get a general idea of where the movie is: towards the top, the middle, or the bottom of your rankings. However, it does not have enough resolution for a continuous ranking, and it is also sensitive to factors like our limited memory, which can only compare it to a few other movies we focus on, instead of the entire rankings. Trying to use a high resolution grading system (like out of a hundred or out of ten with a decimal place) makes these ratings very sensitive. If you rate two movies on two separate days, you may give a 7.8 to film A and 7.5 to film B even if you would have said that you like B better than A on either day. For a movie, we start by asking you to give a star rating out of five. Then, we get a better idea of how much you like it by letting you tell us how it does in one to one matches against other movies.

Enter the Thunderdome

Head to head fights

We need to have high resolution, continuous scores for all movies to be able ro rank them. Directly rating on a high resolution scale is unreliable. Instead, we get to higher resolutions through head to head comparisons. When you determine the winner (or a draw) of a head to head match, the current score of each movie changes according to the result. These scores start with the initial star rating for each movie. Then, how each score changes depends on the previous scores of the competitors. If Casablanca had a score of 5.4, and The Matrix had 4, when you say that The Matrix beats Casablanca, its score goes up quite a bit, whereas Casablanca's score goes down a bit. However, if The Godfather has a score of 4.7, and Troll 2 has a score of 1.4, and The Godfather beats it, the scores don't really change much, because that was already an expected result. Also, the algorithm learns a lot more from the first matches than the hundredth match for a movie, since it already has a lot of information about its true score from the previous matches. Technically, we use the TrueSkill algorithm that we seed by the star ratings as priors. We try to bring you matches that will inform the scores and converge to the true score as fast as possible. On average, the score of a movie converges close to its stable final score in about 10 comparisons.
